# RDR2 Epilogue RPG Overhaul Master Design Draft

## Project Identity
Working title:
John Marston: Ashes of the Frontier

This project is a full epilogue-focused RPG overhaul built entirely inside the existing Red Dead Redemption 2 map.
It does not rely on a brand-new landmass.
Instead, it transforms the epilogue world into a progression-driven frontier RPG with:
- a main storyline
- repeatable content loops
- stats and perk growth
- weapon management and crafting
- gear and accessory progression
- elite enemies and bosses
- world tiers
- family and homestead integration
- long-term endgame farming

## Core Creative Direction
The tone is:
- grounded western first
- frontier horror and myth second
- overt fantasy only in late-game mythic layers

The player fantasy is not "becoming a superhero."
It is John Marston surviving a harsher, stranger, more violent frontier after the epilogue.

## Main Story Premise
After Micah's death, the frontier should have calmed down.
Instead, hidden caches begin surfacing across the West:
- military depots
- gang lockboxes
- abandoned mines
- burned wagons
- forgotten hideouts

These caches contain:
- relics
- charms
- rare weapon parts
- black-market materials
- strange objects with rumor and history attached to them

Organized men begin killing for them.
Mercenaries, bounty crews, smugglers, cultists, outlaw remnants, and relic hunters turn the frontier into a new war zone.

The story begins when Beecher's Hope is hit by an unusually deliberate raid and John discovers a blood-marked ledger tied to the wider network.

## Why RPG Systems Exist In-World

### Accessories
Accessories are frontier objects with reputation and use:
- medals
- badges
- saint tokens
- gambler charms
- trapper talismans
- outlaw trophies

### Upgrades
Upgrades come from:
- gunsmithing
- trapper work
- salvage
- black-market parts
- rare materials
- half-mythic relic binding in late-game content

### Stats
Stats represent John hardening through experience, not abstract game numbers.

### UI
The UI is John's Frontier Ledger, a physical field journal tracking:
- active trails
- danger zones
- bosses
- relics
- progression
- faction knowledge

## World Structure
The project uses only existing locations and reinterprets them.

Main spaces:
- West Elizabeth
- New Austin
- New Hanover
- Lemoyne
- Ambarino

Reused spaces become:
- boss territories
- relic routes
- farming lanes
- outlaw corridors
- elite target zones
- contract hubs

Important anchors:
- Beecher's Hope: main home hub
- Blackwater: first major city node
- Armadillo and Tumbleweed: late frontier pressure zones
- forts, caves, mines, burned farms, and old hideouts: high-value encounter spaces

## Beecher's Hope Hub
Beecher's Hope becomes the heart of progression.

It contains:
- Frontier Ledger table
- workbench
- stash
- target wall
- trophy wall
- map board
- support upgrades

It is both story home and RPG headquarters.

## UI Structure
The UI uses a two-layer model.

### Compact Ledger
Opened anywhere in free roam by hotkey.
Recommended default:
- F10

### Full Ledger
Opened at Beecher's Hope by interacting with the ledger table.

The presentation style should feel like:
- notebook pages
- pinned maps
- portraits
- hand-marked notes

Main tabs:
- Active Trails
- Frontier Map
- Homefront
- Affinity
- John
- Perks
- Gear
- Workshop
- Ledger of the Dead
- Collection

## Core Progression Systems

### Frontier Level
John gains an overall frontier level.

Leveling grants:
- stat points
- perk access
- crafting thresholds
- world progression access

### Base Stats
- Strength
- Grit
- Deadeye
- Survival
- Cunning

These stats affect combat, tracking, trade, recovery, and progression checks.

### Perk Trees
- Gunslinger
- Frontiersman
- Survivor
- Outlaw
- Relic Hunter

Perks can require:
- points
- stat thresholds
- familiarity with weapon types
- boss kills
- story progression
- affinity stage and completed affinity quests

## Weapon Management And Crafting
The weapon system is inspired by Fallout-style ownership, but translated into western language.

### Weapon Layers
- condition
- familiarity
- named weapons
- mod slots
- archetype paths

### Weapon Categories
- revolvers
- pistols
- repeaters
- rifles
- shotguns
- bows

### Core Modding Logic
Each weapon gets limited but meaningful slots rather than infinite modern attachments.

Examples:
- barrel
- frame or receiver
- grip or stock
- sight
- action tune
- ammo preference

### Crafting Stations
- Beecher's Hope: maintenance and core installation
- gunsmith: precision and advanced tuning
- trapper: hunting and wilderness gear
- fence: black-market and relic conversions

### Weapon Progression Fantasy
The player maintains, rebuilds, modifies, and personalizes guns rather than constantly replacing them.

## Gear And Accessories
Equipment stays grounded in Red Dead terms.

Slots include:
- major accessories
- minor charms
- holster mods
- satchel mods
- coat lining
- saddle token

Rarity naming is grounded:
- Worn
- Reliable
- Fine
- Masterwork
- Relic
- Legendary

## Lifestyle Skills
The mod includes non-combat progression as part of the RPG structure.

Lifestyle skills:
- Hunting
- Gunsmithing
- Field Medicine
- Trapping
- Salvaging
- Trade
- Horse Handling
- Homestead

These improve:
- farming efficiency
- crafting access
- support upgrades
- survivability
- economy

## Enemy Structure
Enemies are designed in layers.

### Tiers
- common raiders
- veterans
- enforcers
- named elites
- bosses

### Trait Types
- resistance traits
- penetration traits
- nullify traits
- deflect and ricochet traits
- role-based behavior traits

Examples:
- armored coats reduce revolver damage
- veteran marksmen partially ignore cover bonuses
- elite enemies resist fear and stagger
- certain bosses punish frontal fire until weak states are exposed

Enemies should feel smarter and more dangerous, not merely spongier.

## World Tier System
World Tier is the main difficulty and progression escalator.

Suggested tiers:
- World Tier 1: Frontier Stirring
- World Tier 2: Open Wounds
- World Tier 3: Relic War
- World Tier 4: Ash Frontier
- World Tier 5: After The Ledger

World Tier changes:
- enemy level and density
- trait pools
- loot tables
- boss access
- recipe access
- story gate access

## Story Gating
Main story advancement is not marker-only.
The player must be ready.

Story progression can require:
- minimum level
- stat thresholds
- boss kills
- workbench tier
- relic count
- world tier promotion
- region stabilization

This makes the RPG arc feel earned.

## Existing Mod Integration
Already installed mods are repurposed as official systems inside the overhaul.

- Bounties Expansion: lawful hunting backbone
- Jobs: stable income and household support
- Contracts: surgical black-market path
- Stagecoaches and Money Wagons: supply raid lane
- Duels: reputation and Deadeye lane
- Companion System: temporary support for hard content
- Buyable Properties: regional safehouses and stash nodes
- Bank Robberies: late-game high-risk payout route

These are not treated as isolated mods.
They are integrated into one larger frontier economy.

## Family Integration

### Abigail
Represents cost, stability, and home defense.
Supports:
- medicine
- home upgrades
- supply preparation

### Jack
Acts as the archive keeper.
Supports:
- boss lore
- sketches
- clue interpretation
- ledger updates

### Uncle
Acts as the underworld rumor channel.
Supports:
- black-market rumors
- reckless side work
- dirty shortcuts

The family matters emotionally and mechanically without becoming action companions.

Relationship growth should also sit on top of this layer:

- affinity tracks for Abigail, Jack, and Uncle
- score bars, stage rewards, and dedicated quests
- repeatable payout scaling for high-trust NPCs
- late-stage trust gates that open only once world tiers rise

## Farming Structure
Farming is divided into meaningful lanes.

- Hunter lane
- Raider lane
- Contract lane
- Frontier Work lane
- Boss lane

Every lane feeds visible progression such as:
- upgrades
- perks
- recipes
- home growth
- boss access

The ledger should always tell the player:
- what they are missing
- where it comes from
- what it unlocks next

## Boss Design Direction
Bosses must feel like stories before they feel like stat checks.

Grounded bosses:
- warlords
- elite bounty captains
- smugglers
- ex-military looters

Mythic bosses:
- cult leaders
- impossible beasts
- false legends that become real
- the late-game sky-serpent style threat

The first named boss in the current draft is:
- Silas Redd, The Fence of Ash

The current late-game mastermind concept is:
- The Surveyor

## Ending Structure
The ending must respect canon and keep the sandbox alive.

Final act shape:
- Beecher's Hope comes under real threat
- John uncovers the full network
- the myth thread resolves into a major late-game confrontation
- John ends the frontier war without becoming its ruler

Ending beat:
- Abigail gets one more chance at peace
- Jack keeps the stories
- Uncle keeps the rumors
- John keeps the scars

Post-ending state:
- repeatable content stays active
- bosses can return in hunt form
- world tiers and farming can continue
- Beecher's Hope remains the main hub

## Practical Build Order
The safest real implementation order is:

1. Beecher's Hope hub foundation
2. Compact and full Frontier Ledger
3. level, stats, and perk backbone
4. material and accessory tracking
5. first crafting and weapon tuning layer
6. first integrated repeatable activity loop
7. first elite enemies
8. first boss chain
9. world tier scaling
10. late-game myth arc and ending
